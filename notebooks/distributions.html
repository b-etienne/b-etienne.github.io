Statistical Distributions¶

Probability Density Functions (PDF) and Probability Mass Functions (PMF)¶

PMF: A PMF is a function that gives the probability that a discrete random value is equal to a certain value. It is used to define a discrete probability distribution. If $X$ is a random variable defined on a sample space $S$ (ie $X$ can take its values in $S$), then the PMF is defined as : $$f_{X}(x) = P(X=x)$$

As any probabilistic distribution, the sum of all probabilities is equal to 1, we therefore have $\sum f_{X}(x) = 1$

PDF: A PDF, also called "density", can be seen as the equivalent of a PDF for the case when the random variable is continuous. A random variable $X$ defined on a continuous support has a PDF $f_{x}$ if $P(a\leq X \leq b) = \int_{a}^{b}f_{X}(x)dx$. Once again, don't forget that if $X$ is defined on a support $S$ then $\int_{S}f_{X}(x)dx = 1$.

Question: We consider the distribution for the height (in cm) of a given population. Height, though often given without decimals, is a continuous variable. Assuming that the random variable $X$ representing the height of a individual follows a PDF given by $f_{X}(x)$. What is the probability that an individual has exactly a height $h$ ? Answer: 0. Indeed, we have $P(h\leq X \leq h) = \int_{h}^{h}f_{X}(x)dx = 0$

Discrete distributions¶

Discrete distributions are used when the data takes discrete values, meaning that the sample space (the possible values that a phenomenon or an experiment can take) is discrete. An example of such sample space can be {tails, heads} for a coin tossing, the number given by a dice roll, etc.

We will look at 6 distributions which are the most commonly used ones.

Bernoulli Distribution (Toss a coin: chances of success ?)¶

The Bernoulli distribution is the simplest discrete distribution one can find. The sample space (ie the values our random variable can take) is $\{0, 1\}$, or $\{failure, success\}$. It gives the number of successes of an experiment. If the success probability is $p$, then the probability density function is given by : $$f(0) = 1 - p$$ $$f(1) = p$$ The mean and variance are given by : $$\mu = p$$ $$\sigma^2 = p(1 - p)$$

Binomial Distribution (Toss several coins, how many successes ?) ¶

The Binomial distribution is a n-times repeated Bernoulli experiment. If we consider a Bernoulli experiment indenpendantly repeated n-times, with a sample space equal to {0, 1}, with a success probability of p and k successes, we get the following PMF: $$P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k}$$ $$Mean: \mu = np$$ $$Variance: \sigma^{2} = np(1 - p)$$

Hypergeometric Distribution (Binomial without replacement)¶

The Hypergeometric distribution is very similar to the Binomial distribution except that it is used for a n-times repeated Bernoulli experiment without replacement . The parameters are $N$ the population size, $K$ the size of the population with particular feature, $k$ is the number of successful draws, $n$ is the number of trials : $$PMF : P(X=k)=\frac {\binom{K}{k} \binom{N-K}{n-k}}{\binom{N}{n}}$$ $$Mean: \mu = n\frac{K}{N}$$ $$Variance: \sigma^{2} = n\frac{K}{N}\frac{(N-K)}{N}\frac{(N-n)}{(N-1}$$

Example: Let's play poker. You have in your hand a pair of Jacks, and the first 3 cards have been shown on the table. What are the chances you will get a three-of-a-kind ? A four-of-a-kind ?
Answer: You know the value of 5 cards (2 in your hand, 3 on the table). This leaves $N=52-5=47$ cards unknown. The number of trials is equal to 2 given 2 cards still have to be shown. The total number of Jacks is 4 and you already have 2 Jacks in your hand, hence the value of $K=2$

  • The probability of having a three-of-a-kind (ie having $k=1$ success) is therefore $P(X=1)=\frac {\binom{2}{1} \binom{47-2}{2-1}}{\binom{47}{2}} = 8.326\% $
  • The probability of having a three-of-a-kind (ie having $k=2$ success) is therefore $P(X=2)=\frac {\binom{2}{2} \binom{47-2}{2-2}}{\binom{47}{2}} = 0.095\% $

Geometric Distribution (How many failures before a success ?) ¶

The Geometric distribution models the number of trials before the first success for repeated independant Bernoulli experiments. Imagine tossing a coin and looking at the number k of trials before you get your first successful outcome:
$$PMF: P(X=k)=p(1-p)^{k-1}$$ $$Mean: \mu = \frac{1}{p}$$ $$Variance: \sigma^{2} = \frac{1}{1 - p^{2}}$$

Negative Binomial Distribution (How many failures before k successes ?)¶

The Negative binomial distribution is a generelization of the Geometric distribution. It describes a sequence of $n$ i.i.d. Bernoulli trials, but whereas the Geometric distribution modelled the number of trials before the first success, the Negative Binomial distribution models the numbers of repeated trials until a predefined number $k$ of successes has been reached. A Geometric distribution is therefore a Negative Binomial distribution with $k=1$.
The parameters are $p$ the success probability of a single Bernoulli trial, $k$ the number of failures, and $n$ the number of trials. We get the following PMF: $$P(X=k)=\binom{n+k-1}{k-1}p^{n}(1-p)^{k}$$ $$Mean: \mu = \frac{r}{p}$$ $$Variance: \sigma^{2} = \frac{rq}{1-p^2}$$

If you want to convince yourself about the above results, think about tossing a coin several times. We assume the coin is well balanced and therefore shows a probability $p$ of success equal to 0.5.

In red : what is the probability of having 0 failure before the first success ? Well it is simply the probability to get a success on the first trial, ie 0.5. That's the left-most red column and dot on the graph.

In blue : what is the probability of having 0 failure before 2 successes (ie what is the probability to get 2 successful trials on your 2 first tries ?) ? Assuming that the trials are i.i.d, let's denote $A$ the event "success on the first try" and $B$ "success on the second try". Given that $A$ and $B$ are independant, we get : $$P(A\cap B) = P(A)*P(B)$$ which gives us = 0.5 * 0.5 = 0.25

Poisson Distribution (How many events in a given time interval ?)¶

The Poisson distribution is used to model the number of events occuring during a given time interval. You could think of the number of meteorites hitting the Earth during a 100-year period, the number of calls received on your mobile during a day, etc... The parameter $\lambda$ used in this distribution is the average number of events if the givent time interval. The characteristics of the Poisson distribution are given below: $$PMF: P(X=k)=e^{-\lambda}\frac{\lambda^{k}}{k!}$$ $$Mean: \mu = \lambda$$ $$Variance: \sigma^{2} = \lambda$$

$k$ is a Poisson random variable and the distribution of $k$ follows a Poisson distribution if the following criteria are met:

  • $k$ takes values in $\mathbb{N}$
  • the events are independant, meaning that the occurence of an event does not influence the occurence of following events
  • the rate $\lambda$ is constant (in fact, it is calculated as an average of occuring events across time intervals)
  • two events cannot occur simultaneously, ie you can find a time interval a small as you want between these two events

Example: We suppose that, over a period of 100 years, 1 meteorite hits the Earth on average. What is the probability that at least one meteorite will hit the Earth during the next 100 years ?

Answer : We take a random variable $X$ following a Poisson distribution to model the probabilities of the number of meteorites hitting the Earth in the next 100 years. $\lambda$ is equal to 1. We have $P(X=0)=e^{-\lambda}\frac{\lambda^{0}}{0!} = 0.368$.
The probability of having at least a meteorite hitting the Earth during the next 100 years is therefore $1 - P(X=0) = 0.63$ (remember that $\sum{P(X=k)}=1$)

Exponential Distribution (How much time before an event ?)¶

The Exponential distribution answers the question "How much time before an event". It is often used to compute the reliability of a component given its failure rate. The parameter $\lambda$ used in this distribution is the called the rate and reprensents the skewness of the distribution. The characteristics of the Exponential distribution are given below: $$PDF: f(x;k)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}$$ $$Mean: \mu = \frac {1}{\lambda}$$ $$Variance: \sigma^{2} = \frac {1}{\lambda^{2}}$$

Example: You sell laptops and you want to know how to set the guarantee period.

  • Question 1: You want to know how long it will take so that 50% of your stock (let's assume you sell all of your stock now) is still working ? You also know that your components usually fail after 2 years
  • Question 2: What are the chances that a laptop bought now will still be working after 2 years ?

Answer:

  • We have to solve the equation $P(X \geq k) = 0.5$ with an exponential distribution parametrized by $\lambda=2$. We use the ppf method in scipy to find this value, which is $0.34$ years (3 months).
  • We compute $\int_{0}^{2}{\lambda e^{-\lambda x}}dx$ using the cdf method of our distribution. We get $1-0.9817=1.83\%$ chances that the laptop will still work after 2 years.

Chi-square ($\chi^{2}$) Distribution (Sum of squares)¶

Let's assume you are looking at $n$ variables, each with a mean and a variance such as $X_{i}~N(\mu, \sigma^{2})$. Each of these variables can be standardized to a normally distributed $Z$ random variable via the linear transform $Z_{i} = \frac {(X_{i} - \mu_{i})}{\sigma_{i}}$.
With this, $Z^{2}$ follows a distribution which is called the Chi-square, or $\chi^{2}$, distribution. A Chi-square distibution has a number $n$ of degrees of freedom which corresponds to the number of random variables. Two Chi-square distributions of $m$ and $n$ degrees of freedom can be summed to yield a Chi-square distribution with $n+m$ degrees of freedom.
